Prove Direct Damages from Systemic Negligence

Platform algorithms, market-moving social media posts, and the gap between harm that is real and harm that is legally cognizable. The tobacco playbook translated to the 21st century.

Here is the problem in plain language. A president posts on a social media platform at 3 a.m. that a major trade partner is being cut off. By 9 a.m. when markets open, billions of dollars in pension fund value have evaporated. A retiree in Cleveland loses $40,000 in portfolio value before she can make a single phone call. Is that harm? Absolutely. Can she sue? Almost certainly not — at least not easily, not directly, and not yet.

A technology platform's algorithm is designed to maximize engagement. Engagement is maximized by outrage. Outrage drives polarization. Polarization drives election outcomes. Election outcomes drive policy. Policy drives who is poor and who is not. Is that harm? Yes. Is it a line a court will follow from the platform's A/B testing decisions to a grandmother's Social Security benefit? Not without a great deal of careful, strategic, evidence-based work.

This is the fundamental problem of systemic negligence in the 21st century: the mechanism of harm is institutional and diffuse, the injury is individual and specific, and the legal system was designed for a world where the person who hurt you was standing in the same room.

The tools to close that gap exist. They are not easy. They require a specific kind of preparation. But they have worked before, and they are working now, and this recipe is the map.

Origin Story

The tobacco litigation of the 1990s is the foundational proof of concept for this entire cluster of recipes. For decades, tobacco companies caused mass harm that was individually diffuse — cancer in millions of people who also smoked, also lived near industrial polluters, also had genetic predispositions. The industry's legal defense was built precisely on that diffuseness: you cannot prove my cigarette gave you cancer.

What broke the case was epidemiology, internal documents, and the attorney general theory — not individual lawsuits but state-level actions for aggregate public health costs. The $246 billion Master Settlement Agreement of 1998 was not won by proving that one cigarette caused one cancer. It was won by proving that the industry knew the statistical relationship, concealed it, and the harm to public health systems was directly attributable.

The current social media litigation follows the same playbook. The multi-district litigation in the Northern District of California involving Meta, TikTok, YouTube, and Snap — as of 2024–2025 — is in active discovery. Frances Haugen's 2021 disclosure of Facebook's internal research on Instagram and adolescent mental health was the tobacco-industry-memo moment: proof that the company knew the statistical relationship between its product design and measurable harm, and continued anyway.

The market manipulation cases involving presidential social media posts are at an earlier but structurally identical stage. The precedent is being built right now. This recipe describes the methodology.

Ingredients

Human Ingredients

The Epidemiologist / Statistician

1–2

The most critical ingredient in systemic negligence cases and the one most often missing from early-stage litigation planning. You do not need to prove that this post caused this loss on this day for this plaintiff. You need to prove that a class of posts, issued by a known actor, through a known mechanism, produced a statistically significant and causally attributable pattern of market movement or harm. The expert witness who can do this credibly is the anchor of the entire case.

Temperament: Researcher / Analyst

The Documentary Researcher / FOIA Specialist

2–3

Internal documents are what turn negligence into knowing negligence — and knowing negligence into punitive damages territory. In tobacco it was internal cancer memos. In platform cases it is the internal research (the Haugen disclosures are the template). You need someone who can identify what documents exist, how to get them — through discovery, FOIA, or whistleblowers — and how to use them.

Temperament: Researcher / Analyst

The Individual Plaintiff with Traceable, Specific Harm

1–n

Courts need a named plaintiff. That plaintiff needs: a specific injury, a specific dollar amount or measurable harm, a causal chain that connects their injury to the defendant's conduct, and standing. Finding and preparing named plaintiffs is a distinct skill from litigating the underlying case. Do not underestimate it.

Temperament: Organizer / Connector

The Class Action Specialist

1–2

Systemic negligence at scale almost always needs to be litigated as a class action or MDL. The procedural mechanics of class certification — proving individual questions don't predominate, that the class representative is adequate, that the class is ascertainable — are a specialty unto themselves. You need this person early, not after the complaint is filed.

Temperament: Litigator / Legal

The Financial / Market Expert

1–2

In market manipulation cases: an expert who can model the counterfactual market (what would have happened absent the post), the causal relationship between the post and the movement, the portion of the movement attributable to the specific conduct versus confounding factors, and the distribution of harm across the affected population. Forensic economics, recognized and court-accepted.

Temperament: Researcher / Analyst

The Steps

Step 1 · 3–6 months

Identify and Document the Specific Mechanism of Harm

Systemic negligence cases fail most often because plaintiffs describe the harm in general terms without specifying the mechanism. Before you file anything, you need a theory of causation that goes: specific actor → specific conduct → specific mechanism → measurable harm at the individual level. For market manipulation by social media post, this means: account → post at timestamp X → algorithmic amplification → retail investor panic selling → demonstrable price movement → quantifiable portfolio loss in identifiable class. Each arrow in that chain needs evidence.

Pro tip: Map the causal chain on a whiteboard before you research it in case law. If you can't draw the arrows, you can't argue them.

Step 2 · Immediate and ongoing

Preserve the Evidence Before It Disappears

Social media posts get deleted. Accounts get suspended. Platforms update terms of service in ways that affect data access. Archive everything the moment it happens: the posts, the timestamps, the engagement metrics if accessible, the market data from the same time window. Evidence preservation is not a later step — it is Step 2 because the evidence is perishable.

Step 3 · 6–18 months

Build the Statistical Case Before You File

In tobacco litigation the epidemiological case was substantially built before the state AGs filed. The Haugen disclosure came before the wave of state AG suits in platform cases. You want your expert's event study, your statistical model of harm distribution, and your causal chain documented and peer-reviewed before opposing counsel sees your complaint. The complaint is the end of the research phase, not the beginning.

Pro tip: The academic literature on social media and market effects is growing rapidly. Run a SSRN search for 'social media market impact event study' — your expert's methodology should be grounded in published, peer-reviewed work.

Step 4 · 3–6 months

Find Your Named Plaintiffs and Prepare Them

A named plaintiff in a class action or MDL needs to understand that they are the public face of a years-long proceeding. They need a specific, documented injury. In market manipulation cases, the ideal named plaintiff is a retail investor with a brokerage account showing specific trades during the window of the alleged manipulation, resulting in documented losses. Not a hedge fund. Not an institution. A person.

Step 5 · 1–2 months

Choose Your Theory of Liability Carefully

You have options: Negligence (duty, breach, causation, damages — note: platforms have argued Section 230 immunity, but that immunity is not absolute and is under judicial pressure); Negligence per se (if the conduct violated a statute, e.g. securities fraud statutes for market-moving posts); Products liability (courts are increasingly receptive to social media algorithms as 'defective products'); Public nuisance (worked in opioid litigation, being tested in platform cases). Each theory has different evidentiary requirements and damages frameworks. Choose before you research, not after.

Pro tip: The products liability theory — treating the algorithm as a defective product — is the most innovative and the most promising for platform cases as of 2025. Study the current MDL orders for the most current judicial posture.

Step 6 · 1–3 months

Plead Specific Damages with Specific Numbers

'I was harmed' is not enough. 'I suffered $40,000 in portfolio losses between 9:31 a.m. and 11:45 a.m. on [date], traceable to market movement directly caused by [specific post] at [specific timestamp], as documented by [brokerage records]' is a cognizable damages allegation. The specificity is not just good practice — it is the difference between a complaint that survives a motion to dismiss and one that doesn't.

Step 7 · 12–36 months

Use Discovery Aggressively to Build the Systemic Case

Once in litigation, discovery is your primary tool for converting systemic harm from allegation to evidence. In platform cases: internal research on algorithm design and known harms, communications between executives about the safety/engagement tradeoff, data on post amplification patterns. In market manipulation cases: communications between the account holder and anyone with market positions, trading records in the window around posts, prior similar instances. The pattern is as important as the incident.

Step 8 · 6–18 months

Connect Individual Harm to Aggregate Pattern for Class Certification

To certify a class, you need to prove that common questions predominate. The way you do this in systemic negligence cases is by showing that the mechanism of harm is uniform even if individual injury amounts vary. Every member of the class was harmed by the same conduct through the same mechanism — only the dollar amounts differ. The statistical model is what makes this argument.

Pro tip: Class certification is often where these cases are won or lost. Study the most recent certification orders in the social media MDL carefully — they're public on PACER.

Frequently Asked Questions & Troubleshooting

What about Section 230 immunity for platforms?▾
Section 230 immunizes platforms from liability for third-party content. It does not immunize them for their own algorithmic design choices — the decision to amplify certain content, to recommend certain content, to design for maximum engagement regardless of harm. The current MDL is substantially built on this distinction, and courts have been increasingly willing to let it proceed. Section 230 is a defense, not an absolute bar.
What about the political speech problem — don't social media posts by politicians get First Amendment protection?▾
The First Amendment protects speech from government censorship — it does not protect a speaker from civil liability for damages caused by their speech in all circumstances. Fraud, market manipulation, incitement to imminent lawless action, and other categories have long been held to be outside First Amendment protection. The key is the specific legal theory: speech that is also securities fraud is still securities fraud.
What about the proximate causation problem — doesn't the market always move for multiple reasons at once?▾
Yes, and this is where the event study methodology does its work. An event study isolates the market impact of a specific event from baseline noise, prior trends, and confounding factors. It produces a dollar estimate of the abnormal return attributable to the specific event. It is the standard methodology in securities litigation and has been accepted by courts for decades. The challenge in novel cases is not the methodology — it is finding the right expert and the quality of the data.
What if the defendant is the government, not a private actor?▾
Government liability is substantially harder. Sovereign immunity limits suits against the federal government to cases where Congress has specifically waived immunity. The Federal Tort Claims Act waives immunity for some negligent acts but excludes 'discretionary functions' — meaning policy decisions. The better theory against government actors is usually constitutional (due process, equal protection, takings clause) rather than tort.
What kills these cases?▾
In roughly descending order: failure to preserve evidence early (perishable digital evidence is gone before discovery), a weak or underprepared expert (the statistical case has to survive a Daubert challenge), poor named plaintiff selection (institutional investors don't make sympathetic class representatives), and Section 230 applied too broadly by a district court. The antidote to each is built into the steps above.
