Amend the Constitution

Two paths. Thirty-eight states. Twenty-seven times it has been done. Here is the full operational map from proposal to ratification — with the ERA as proof that even a 202-year-old amendment can be activated.

You have been told, in approximately a thousand different ways, that amending the Constitution is impossible. You have been told this by political scientists, by pundits, by well-meaning civics teachers, and by the general atmosphere of a country that has not ratified a new amendment since 1992 — and that one, the 27th, was originally proposed in 1789.

They are not lying to you. It is extraordinarily difficult.

They are also, in a specific and important way, wrong.

The Constitution has been amended 27 times. Nearly 12,000 amendments have been proposed since 1789. Twenty-seven have been ratified. That is not a failure rate. It is a filter. The question is not whether the recipe can produce the dish. It can. The question is whether you are prepared to cook at this temperature, for this long, with this many cooks in the kitchen.

Origin Story

The 27th Amendment — which prevents Congress from giving itself a mid-term pay raise — was proposed by James Madison in 1789 and ratified in 1992. It sat dormant for 202 years before a University of Texas undergraduate named Gregory Watson discovered it, wrote a paper about it, got a C, and then spent the next decade convincing state legislatures to ratify it anyway. He eventually got 38 states. His professor changed his grade to an A in 2017.

This happened. The amendment process is not a closed system. It is a patient one.

The Equal Rights Amendment has an equally instructive arc. Congress passed it in 1972. It needed 38 states to ratify; it got 35 by 1979, then stalled. The deadline was extended to 1982. The campaign went cold for decades. Then, between 2017 and 2020, Nevada, Illinois, and Virginia became states 36, 37, and 38. The ERA now has its 38 ratifications — and a live federal lawsuit over whether the Archivist of the United States is obligated to certify it. The recipe didn't fail. It went into a long simmer.

Before that: the 26th Amendment, giving 18-year-olds the vote, was ratified in 100 days — the fastest in history — because the anti-Vietnam War movement had spent years building exactly the public pressure needed to move it that fast when the political moment arrived. The arc from 11 states to 38 is not a single moment. It is 27 sequential moments, each requiring its own campaign.

Ingredients

Human Ingredients

The Constitutional Scholar

1–2

At least one person who has read Article V more than once and understands the legal landscape — what 'proposal' requires, what 'ratification' requires, where the genuine legal ambiguities live. The ERA ratification debate turned substantially on whether a state can rescind its ratification, and that question is still unresolved. This person does not need to be a law professor. They need to be thorough, rigorous, and unafraid of primary sources.

Temperament: Litigator / Legal

The Legislative Strategist

2–4

Someone who understands how Congress actually works — not the textbook version, the real one. Who are the persuadable votes in both chambers? What does a two-thirds majority coalition actually look like, and what does it cost to build? If going the National Convention route, this person needs to understand state legislative politics in 34 different states simultaneously.

Temperament: Organizer / Connector

The Long-Arc Organizer

3–6

The most undervalued ingredient in this recipe. Constitutional amendments are not won in a single session of Congress. They are won by people who build and sustain coalitions across years and decades, who bring new people in when old people burn out, who keep the goal alive through hostile, indifferent, and supportive administrations alike. This person is the keeper of the flame. Without them, the recipe goes cold.

Temperament: Organizer / Connector

The Storyteller / Public Communicator

2–4

Public opinion shapes ratification. States ratify what their constituents want ratified. The suffragists understood this. The story of why this amendment has to be legible to a grandmother in Ohio, a college student in Texas, and a veteran in Georgia. Not one story — many stories, in many registers, for many audiences.

Temperament: Storyteller / Communicator

The Funder / Resource Architect

1–2

A multi-year, multi-state constitutional amendment campaign costs money. Someone needs to build and steward that infrastructure. The ERA was substantially advanced by grassroots organizing — PAC money is not required — but sustained, reliable resource commitment is.

Temperament: Funder / Resource Architect

The Legal Watchdog

1–2

Constitutional challenges will come. The ERA's path to ratification included a federal lawsuit about whether the 1982 deadline was constitutionally valid. These fights are real and require real legal capacity, built in advance — not assembled in response.

Temperament: Litigator / Legal

The Steps

Step 1 · 1–3 months

Choose Your Amendment and Your Path

Before anything else: what does the amendment say, and how are you proposing to get it there? The Congressional path (two-thirds of both chambers) is the only path that has ever successfully produced a ratified amendment. The National Convention path (applications from 34 state legislatures) has never been used and carries significant legal uncertainty about what a convention could or couldn't do once convened. Know which kitchen you're cooking in.

Pro tip: If your amendment is already proposed and circulating in Congress, join that effort before starting a new one. The 27th Amendment waited 202 years — it didn't need a new proposal, just ratification.

Step 2 · 2–4 weeks

Find the Unfinished Recipes Already in the Oven

Six proposed amendments are currently awaiting state ratification. One dates to 1789. The Equal Rights Amendment has 38 ratifications and a live legal dispute about whether it is already part of the Constitution. Before you start from scratch, check what's already on the stove. Joining an active ratification effort is faster and more efficient than proposing a new amendment.

Pro tip: The Congressional Apportionment Amendment of 1789 has never formally expired. Neither has the Child Labor Amendment of 1924. These are legally dormant but not dead.

Step 3 · 2–6 months

Map the 67 + 38 Coalition

Two-thirds of both chambers to propose. Three-fourths of states to ratify. In concrete numbers: 290 House votes, 67 Senate votes, 38 state legislatures. Build your map. Who are you starting with? Who are the persuadables? Who is structurally opposed and why? The ERA spent years identifying the 15 states that hadn't ratified and building campaigns in each one. Start with the same discipline.

Pro tip: Use GovTrack to model which legislators have voted for similar measures in the past. Your list of persuadables is smaller than it looks — and more winnable.

Step 4 · 6–18 months per state

Build the State-Level Infrastructure First

Ratification happens in state legislatures. The national campaign sets the frame, but the ground game is local. In each target state you need: a lead organization with credibility in that state, a legislative champion in both chambers, a constituent mobilization capacity, and a legal readiness plan. This is not work that can be done from Washington.

Step 5 · Ongoing from Step 3

Run the Public Opinion Campaign in Parallel

Legislators follow constituents when constituents are loud, organized, and sustained. The story of your amendment needs to be told in local terms, in local media, in the language of the communities most affected. Not one national narrative — 38 state-specific ones built on the same foundation.

Pro tip: The suffragists used a state-by-state strategy that built public support and legislative momentum simultaneously. Don't wait for legislative wins to start the narrative campaign.

Step 6 · Varies by state

Lock In Ratifications One at a Time

Each state ratification is a win. Treat it as one. Each win builds momentum for the next, changes the media narrative, puts pressure on remaining states, and gives your coalition something to celebrate and recruit around. The arc from 11 states to 38 is not a single moment — it is 27 sequential moments.

Step 7 · Ongoing

Defend Against Legal Challenges at Every Stage

Someone will challenge the timeline. Someone will argue that rescissions are valid. Someone will file suit to block certification. Build your legal defense in advance, not in response. Know the legal landscape cold before you hit Step 6.

Pro tip: The ERA legal defense strategy is the current model. Study it: ACLU, ERAcoalition.org, and the federal litigation record are all public.

Step 8 · 3–6 months

When You Get to 38, Be Ready to Certify

The Archivist of the United States officially certifies ratification. The Director of the Federal Register processes the documents. Know this process. Have your documentation in order. The 27th Amendment was certified in 1992 in a formal ceremony. Yours should be too — which means your legal team needs to have prepared the certification argument before the 38th ratification happens, not after.

Frequently Asked Questions & Troubleshooting

Can a state rescind its ratification?▾
This is genuinely unsettled constitutional law. Several states have attempted to rescind ERA ratifications and Congress has disputed their validity. The short answer: it has never been definitively adjudicated. Your legal team needs a documented position on this before you reach Step 6.
What if Congress sets a ratification deadline and we miss it?▾
The ERA deadline dispute is the instructive precedent. The original 1979 deadline was extended to 1982 — itself a contested action. Whether Congress can extend or remove a deadline, and whether states that ratified after a deadline are legally counted, remains in active litigation. Don't assume the deadline is the finish line.
What's the difference between the Congressional path and the National Convention path?▾
Congress has proposed all 27 ratified amendments. The Convention path has never been used and has generated serious debate about whether a convention could be limited in scope or might open the entire Constitution for revision. This is not a theoretical concern — it is a strategic one with significant risk. Most constitutional scholars advise against the Convention path for this reason.
What kills amendment campaigns?▾
In roughly descending order: loss of a key legislative champion, funding exhaustion, coalition fracture over tactics or messaging, a hostile federal administration actively campaigning against ratification, and slow erosion of urgency when progress stalls. The antidote to each is the same: deep, distributed, institutionally embedded organizing that does not depend on any single leader, funder, or political moment.
What if we don't have 67 Senate votes?▾
You may not need them all at once. The 26th Amendment passed in 100 days because years of organizing had already built the pressure. Your job is to build the organizing infrastructure and the public pressure so that when the political moment arrives — and it will arrive — you can move fast. The Gregory Watson story is the model: one person, correct information, 10 years of sustained effort, one ratified amendment.
