The founding frame

Hope Make It Not Ashamed

There is a sentence in a 2022 philosophy paper by John Danaher that I keep returning to. He is drawing on an instrumentalist understanding of what technology is, and he writes that 'technology is thus understood to be a set of tools that humans use to solve problems and achieve goals.' Simple enough. But then I ask: what is my problem? What is my goal?

I am a writer. My problem is time and space — the gap between the words I carry inside and the world that needs to receive them. My goal, if I am being honest with myself, is not merely expression. It is reach. It is income. It is the possibility that an idea, formed in private, might travel far enough to matter to someone I will never meet. That is the real project. And artificial intelligence, as I understand it now, is a tool that can work on that problem, in service of that goal. Not a replacement for the writer. An instrument in the writer's hand.

We are living through a moment when techno-optimism, which ought to belong to all of us, has been effectively captured by a narrative that belongs to almost no one. The headlines are about IPOs. About which model is larger than the last one. About what jobs AI will eliminate, and when, and whose. The replacement theory fears — which were already woven into our social fabric long before any chatbot was launched — have found new oxygen.

We have been here before. The dot-com bubble did not destroy the internet — it destroyed the illusion that access to the technology was the same thing as access to the benefit. What has arrived, reliably, is the extraction. The attention. The data. The engagement. That is what happens when the mechanism by which we select which ideas get built and why is handed entirely to the market.

The question I am trying to answer — and that I believe more of us are quietly trying to answer — is whether it is possible to hold a personal techno-optimism that does not require surrendering to impersonal techno-determinism. Whether the individual can orient toward AI as a genuine tool for genuine ends without simply becoming another unit of engagement in someone else's extraction economy.

What I am proposing is a shift in orientation. From user to operator. From passive recipient of technology to active agent within it. A user consumes. An operator shapes. A user is acted upon by the platform's design. An operator asks: what do I want this to do, and how do I want it done, and in whose interest? The user-to-operator shift is not a matter of technical skill. It is a matter of posture. It is, at its root, a matter of belief — specifically, the belief that you are permitted to have a relationship with technology on your own terms.

There is, underneath all of this, something I can only call a theological instinct. Not a doctrine, but an orientation. The conviction that to intend goodness — genuinely, not performatively — is itself a form of participation in something that moves toward goodness. That hope, in this specific sense, is not passive. It is active. It acts on the world. And it is not ashamed.

Paul wrote, in a letter that has outlasted every empire it was written inside of: 'hope maketh not ashamed.' The hope he was describing was not optimism about outcomes. It was confidence in direction — a trust that the orientation itself is sound, even when the destination is not yet visible. That is the kind of techno-optimism I am reaching for. Not the certainty that AI will make everything better, but the conviction that human beings, individually and together, can use it with enough intention and enough mutual regard to make it matter.

That is the long view. That is the opening.

— Penny Wrenn. April 13, 2026.
