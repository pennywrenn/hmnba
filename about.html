The site

About

5.1

What This Is

Hope Make It Not Ashamed (HMNBA) is a civic action recipe blog. It is modeled structurally on food recipe blogs — specifically on the architecture of sites like Pinch of Yum, which organize complex processes into warm, accessible, illustrated, step-by-step formats that assume the reader is capable but not yet practiced. The translation is exact: where a food blog teaches you to make bread, HMNBA teaches you to change a law. Where a food blog gives you the ingredients for soup, HMNBA gives you the ingredients for a winning legal theory.

The site operates at the intersection of four things that are almost never combined in a single resource: philosophical grounding (the long view on technology, optimism, and human agency), legal and civic education (how the systems actually work, not how civics class said they work), operational specificity (exact steps, exact tools, exact timelines, exact temperaments required), and real-world proof (every recipe is anchored in an outcome that has already occurred somewhere).

The animating belief of the site is this: the playbook has always existed. Thurgood Marshall had one. The architects of Dobbs had one. The drafters of Project 2025 published theirs openly. The asymmetry in American civic life is not that one side has a playbook and the other doesn’t. It is that one side has been cooking from written recipes for a century while the other side has been improvising from vibes and good intentions. HMNBA is the written recipe.

The site does not tell you what to believe. It tells you how to do what you already believe needs doing.

The shift is from user to operator. A user participates in the systems as they are designed. An operator asks different questions: What lever produces what outcome? What is the sequence of operations? Who needs to be in the room at which step? What has this produced before, where, and under what conditions?

5.2

How the Recipes Are Made

Every recipe begins with a documented real-world outcome — a law that passed, a case that was won, an institution that was changed. The outcome is identified through AI-assisted research of legislative databases, court records, news archives, and organizational reports. The research produces a raw evidence file: a timeline of the outcome, the key actors, the documented steps they took, the resources they used, and the obstacles they faced.

The raw evidence file is processed by the editorial team, who reverse-engineer it into recipe format: identifying the steps in sequence, naming the human ingredients required at each step, cataloging the technology tools used, and writing the origin story from the evidence. The recipe is then reviewed by at least one person with direct experience in the issue area — either someone who participated in a similar effort or a subject matter expert who can validate the accuracy of the steps and the completeness of the troubleshooting section.

Before publication, each recipe is checked against three questions:

  • Is the outcome real and documented?
  • Are the steps accurate and complete?
  • Is the difficulty rating honest — does the recipe acknowledge what is genuinely hard, not just what is theoretically possible?

After publication, recipes are continuously revised based on the review system. A recipe is a living document, not a finished product. A recipe that consistently generates critical reviews at Step 4 gets a Step 4 revision.

5.3

How the Rating System Works

The rating system measures one thing: operational usefulness. Did this recipe help you do the thing? Not: do you agree with the goal. Not: is the issue important. Those are questions the reader answers for themselves before they pick up the recipe. The rating is purely about whether the recipe worked as a recipe.

★★★★★

I used this recipe, I followed the steps, and it produced something close to the described yield.

★★★★☆

I used this recipe and it worked but with significant modification at one or more steps — here's what I changed and why.

★★★☆☆

I used this recipe and it helped me understand the territory even though my outcome was different — here's why.

★★☆☆☆

I used this recipe and the steps were incomplete or inaccurate in ways that cost me significant time — here's what was wrong.

★☆☆☆☆

This recipe should not be published as written — here's what needs to change.

Reviews without a written explanation are not weighted in the aggregate rating. The review system is the site’s quality control mechanism and its primary source of continuous improvement.

5.4

Who This Is For

The site is explicitly for people who already have a goal and need operational knowledge to pursue it. It is not for people who are looking to be told what goals to have.

The site does not endorse specific political parties, candidates, or ideological positions. It documents and operationalizes outcomes that have been achieved through legal, civic, and democratic processes. The decision of which outcomes to pursue belongs entirely to the reader.

That said: the site is not neutral on the question of whether people have the right to use civic and legal processes to pursue change. It is emphatically not neutral on that. The premise of the site is that those processes belong to everyone, that the knowledge of how to use them should be freely available, and that the asymmetry between those who have been cooking from written recipes and those who have been improvising is a problem worth solving.

The site is for the ERA advocate who has been at this for a decade and the 22-year-old who just learned that six proposed amendments are still technically pending. For the tenant organizer building their first coalition and the litigator filing their fifth MDL complaint. For anyone who has ever said "someone should do something about this" and is ready to become that someone.

Let them cook.
